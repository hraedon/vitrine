Walk the composite household

The home and the four people in it, across the century. The argument isn't narrated — it's in the sourced facts: watch unpaid labour fall as comforts arrive, and the house grow from cramped to spacious.

This is not a family, and it was never this small. Each figure is a stack of separate medians. The point of the walk is what nostalgia forgets: the single-earner past was materially spartan, and the technology that filled these rooms is what freed a woman from a lifetime of grinding, unpaid work. Reading the walk

Falling bars (labour) shown in a warmer tone; rising bars (comforts, income, size) in brass. Absent technology isn't drawn — a bare house says more than ghosts.

Where a figure isn't sourced yet, it shows as a gap or a flagged provisional value, never a confident guess.

About this demo. Populated figures are drawn verbatim from vitrine's US rooms. The mother's unpaid-labour hours are provisional pending extraction of Ramey (2009); the modern point anchors on the 2020s ATUS fact already in the corpus (all-adult household activities — a concept-splice from Ramey's women's series, flagged not smoothed). Floor areas are provisional (new-construction proxy). Food's share of spending and life expectancy are fully sourced. The house scale is indicative; the square-foot figure is the datum.
