## On the project

vitrine's provenance gate continues to be its strongest structural
asset. The "no quantity → render as gap" contract means concept splices
are visible, not hidden. But the ATUS/Ramey splice exposed a genuine
tension in the fact model: the `quantity` field is both a data-model
property (does the fact have a chartable number?) and a rendering
directive (should the arc chart show a dot or a gap?). When a fact has
a real number but in incompatible units (hrs/day vs hrs/week), setting
quantity is correct per the model but produces a visually misleading
chart point. The arc caveat is the only mitigation, and it relies on
the visitor reading it.

The agent-notes lifecycle transition bug (in_review → closed not
supported; close command crashes with "fold_work_item returned None")
is annoying but workaround-able via review pass + accept. The
reconcile feature also failed on the same transition. These are
agent-notes bugs, not vitrine bugs.

## On the work done

**What went well:** The ATUS data was a clean win — born-digital PDFs
with text layers extractable via pypdf, no OCR needed. The A-2
weekday/weekend cross-verification reconciled to the A-1 overall
average exactly (2.144 ≈ 2.14 for women, 1.420 = 1.42 for men), giving
high confidence in the transcription. The vehicle-arc tests were
straightforward to write following the existing `test_visualization.py`
patterns.

**What was awkward:** The initial WI-006 implementation omitted
`quantity` from the ATUS facts, treating it as a rendering concern.
The Nemotron reviewer correctly caught this as a fact-model violation
(facts with a single headline number must carry a quantity). Fixing it
required updating the arc caveats to explain the visual discontinuity
instead of relying on gap rendering. The second-round Kimi reviewer
then caught that the men's arc was missing the 2020s splice point —
a symmetry issue I hadn't noticed. Two rounds of review, three fixes,
and the result is meaningfully better than the initial implementation.

**What I'm confident in:** The ATUS transcription is verified by
internal arithmetic (A-2 weighted average = A-1 overall). The
vehicle-arc tests are correct and follow existing conventions. The
README status update (307→313 facts, 18→16 gaps) is accurate.

**What I'd want a second pair of eyes on:** The arc chart now plots
2.14 (hrs/day) on the same y-axis as 29.3 (hrs/week) for the
home-production arcs. The caveat explains this, but a visitor who
skips the caveat sees a 93% drop from 2000s to 2010s. Is this better
or worse than rendering as a gap? I believe it's better (the data is
real, the caveat flags the splice), but the visual is jarring.
