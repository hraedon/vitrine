## On the project

vitrine's mechanical provenance gate continues to be its strongest asset.
The gate caught the 1910s-1930s priced-fact-without-anchor issue immediately
— I had to remove `amount_minor` from those facts because the rooms lack
income/wage anchors. This is correct behavior: the affordability corridor
can't compute a ratio without a denominator, so the price is displayed in
the room but the corridor renders the gap. The "render the gap" ethos is
load-bearing and well-enforced.

The corpus is now at 312 facts (after merging with the remote's 5 new
budget-panel facts). The visualization layer is mature — adding facts to
existing arcs and affordability corridors requires only data file edits and
curation.py updates. The mark-coverage gate ensures nothing renders without
a curated fact behind it.

## On the work done

**What went well:** The confirmation pass was thorough. Three subagents
verified 47 facts against primary sources (F-8 Excel, Census housing text
files, BLS Bulletin 1312-12 PDF page 287) with zero discrepancies. The
vehicle ownership arc expansion was a clean win — the data existed in the
corpus but the curation only referenced 2 of 7 decades. The BEA transaction
price data (ORNL TEDB Table 11.13) is an excellent Tier A source for
1970s-2020s car prices.

**What was awkward:** The 1970s vehicle ownership fact used CEX (80.1%,
"families") while all other decades used BTS/Census (housing units). The
subagent verification caught this. Switching to BTS (82.5%) was the right
call for series consistency, but it meant discarding the richer CEX data
(income breakdown). The CEX figure is preserved in the notes.

**What I'm confident in:** The BEA transaction price facts (1970s-2020s)
are directly transcribed from the source — no computation needed. The
vehicle arc expansion is straightforward. The test fix (stale reference to
renamed fact) was caught by both adversarial reviewers.

**What I'd want a second pair of eyes on:** The Historical Statistics
wholesale averages (1910s-1960s) are computed from total wholesale value ÷
number of cars. The adversarial reviewers flagged this as potential "cross-
fact arithmetic in data files." I believe it's acceptable because: (1) both
numbers come from the same source table (Series Q 148-162), not from
different facts; (2) the computation is simple division, not estimation; (3)
the existing 1950s car-price fact already does this in its notes; (4) the
value field explicitly says "computed from factory sales." But the charter
is strict about arithmetic, and a future session might want to convert these
to `[[derived]]` facts with structured operands.

## Gaps to flag

- **Wholesale/transaction price mix in affordability corridor** — the
  "A new car" line now spans 1900s-2020s with three different price
  methodologies. No corridor-level caveat. The jump from 1960s ($1,822
  wholesale) to 1970s ($3,543 transaction) partly reflects methodology, not
  just real price change. (`src/vitrine/site/curation.py:328` AFFORD_ITEMS)

- **1910s-1930s car-price facts have no `amount_minor`** — they display in
  the room but don't appear in the affordability corridor. This is correct
  (no anchors) but means the corridor has a gap for those decades even though
  car price data exists. A future session could add wage anchors to these
  rooms using the manufacturing wage data.

- **BEA transaction price series ends in 2020** — the 2020s room uses a 2020
  price ($27,366) against a 2024 wage. Industry sources report ~$47,000+
  by 2023, but those include SUVs/trucks. The 4-year gap triggers a year-gap
  caveat in the comparison, which is correct.

- **No test for the expanded vehicle arc** — no test asserts the vehicle arc
  has 7 points or that the automobile stage glyph renders for 1960s-2020s.
  The mark-coverage gate provides indirect assurance, but a targeted test
  would make the contract explicit.
